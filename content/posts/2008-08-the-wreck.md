## Contextless thoughts

I enjoy my extended family without the burden of my immediate family.

Relive today. I woke up at 10:00 naturally, 9 hours of sleep was enough because I got enough nutrients and figured out whatever else the problem was. I went to Allegro with a mission, and flowed around the obstacles as they occurred in this mission. Then, I took my clean motorcycle with all things working down to Puyallup for the tutorial. I brought along snacks that I liked, so I did not have to purchase fast food. Once there I didn't purchase anything, I simply earned my 40 dollars and left. Without forgetting anything, because I didn't have much to forget. Once I came here, there still wasn't internet, but I knew there were elements of language studying that did not require internet to be done, so I worked on those. I still purchased the coffee, because it was cold out and I wanted to feel good while I taught, but I started my day somewhere different than Allegro, and so this was my first coffee of the day.

## Act I: The Slide

I want to relive the moment, play by play. First off, it is more than wet. The air is like a series of suspended raindrops, half water, half air. I came off I-5 needing gas. There below me on the right is a shell station, which is great as I slow down—but then, coming in off another exit is a car in the lane that I want to go in. I speed up to pass them, but they are accelerating too. There's not enough time to out accelerate them, considering that I have to be going slow for the right hand turn. So I slow down to get behind them and not overrun the brakes. At the same time, I'm leaning to the left with the intention of staying away from the car. I was clear in my intentions, the blinker had been on the entire time. They should have known. But then as I was slowing down, there was not enough traction.

I'm on the ground sliding along the pavement. More speed but less incline and the same wetness as a waterslide. I'm not with the bike anymore, but I'm still moving with speed. ROLL! I tell myself. It takes a bit but even with my backpack strapped on with teaching material and my laptop, I manage a revolution. I slide another distance and come to a stop. There's no pain, and I don't even do a mental check of injuries, there are none. I'm looking forward, and the car responsible has stopped and is certainly watching while I get up. Shit! I think to myself, what happens if the bike is unrepairable? What happens if my laptop is busted? Nobody will hear about this.

## Act II: Damage Report

I walked back to the bike, which I remember glimpsing as I rolled with sparks coming as it slid down the pavement. I stood and looked over it, the rear wheel still moving and in gear like an insect twitching after death. There was a pang of guilt. I hauled it up, with my shoes squishing from being so waterlogged, and flip the red switch to turn it off. I rolled it to the side of the roadway, cringing with every complaint from the chain as the bike was still in gear. I can't find the clutch lever, I see the left mirror on the sparkling black pavement, and now that I know I am OK and the bike is OK, wonder how I am going to get home.

A bystander from the gas station above who saw the incident motions at me and inquiringly gives the thumbs up signal. I give two thumbs up in reply. I'm fine. The car that blocked me is still waiting there at the intersection. I don't care about them, I care about my bike. I say aloud to nobody but myself in a cavalier tone "What's the damage? Front turn signal cover. Rear turn signal bent. Left mirror and gear shift lever need to be replaced." The car, seeing me walking, drives off. I think to myself, "that's odd. I would not drive away if I just witnessed/caused an accident."

## Act III: The Squeegee Repair

The bystander is still there, and before she has a chance to speak I tell her "that must not have been fun to watch." "Are you sure you're OK?" I'm feeling around my pockets at this time for my debit card. She clearly wants to help, but there isn't much she can do. "What happened with the other car? They just drove off?" She even offers to pay my gas, but I assure her I have money for that and that it's not a problem.

I walk in, and ask for Band-Aids, disinfectant, and a pair of pliers. I'm reminded of a scene from the Bourne Supremacy although luckily this time I'm not running from cops. They don't have pliers. So to fix the gear shift lever, I have to use the window squeegee. It works well enough. I unscrew the useless mirror and throw it into the trash bin full of candy wrappers and soda cans.

40 miles home.
